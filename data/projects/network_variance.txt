Many real-world systems combine two kinds of information: a network structure and data distributed over that network. Scientific concepts are connected through relations of meaning, brain regions through anatomy, places through mobility, and social groups through interaction. In this project, we develop a way to measure the variance and covariance of distributions on networks while taking the distance between nodes into account.

The main application is a “network of knowledge” built from mathematical concepts linked through Wikipedia. Using more than 140,000 arXiv papers, we study how mathematical ideas appear and co-appear in scientific writing. The results show that papers tend to use concepts that are closer together in the knowledge network than expected by chance, while co-occurring concepts have higher network covariance than in randomized baselines. In other words, the geometry of the knowledge network helps reveal how mathematical ideas are combined in practice.

The same framework can be used more broadly whenever signals or distributions live on top of a network: to compare structural and functional brain networks, measure economic diversity, study polarization in social networks, or identify peripheral and boundary-like regions of graphs through maximum-variance distributions.
