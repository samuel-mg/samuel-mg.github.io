Can large language models behave like humans in strategic social situations? This project explores how LLMs can complement traditional behavioral experiments: first by reproducing known human patterns, then by generating testable predictions for new games that can be validated in future human experiments.

We test this by asking several open LLMs to play the same game-theoretic experiments previously conducted with human participants. The results show that models differ sharply: Llama closely reproduces aggregate human cooperation patterns, Qwen behaves much more like a Nash-equilibrium reasoner, and Mistral shows a noisier, more optimistic and trustful profile. We also find that robust prompting is essential: simple direct answers can look almost random, while structured prompting and logical verification reveal meaningful behavioral patterns. Looking inside the models, attention analyses suggest that Llama processes payoff information in a more structured way, which may help explain why it aligns better with human behavior.
