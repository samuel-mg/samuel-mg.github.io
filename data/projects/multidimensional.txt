Our social ties are shaped by several identity dimensions at once: ethnicity, gender, age, education, socioeconomic background, and more. In this line of research, we develop MAPS — Multidimensional Aggregation of Social Preferences — a model to study how these dimensions combine to structure real social networks. MAPS suggests that people’s connection choices are well described by a simple mechanism: they evaluate identity dimensions separately, and ties are most likely when all relevant evaluations are favorable. This allows us to recover interpretable preferences in systems such as school friendships and marriages, from ethnic and age homophily to aspirational patterns across grades or socioeconomic groups.

The same multidimensional logic can also create unexpected tie inequalities: systematic differences in quantity and quality of connections across social groups. Even when preferences are independent across identity dimensions, the social advantages and disadvantages that emerge in the network are not. Group size imbalances, homophily, and correlations between attributes can produce intersectional patterns that cannot be reduced to simple one-dimensional effects. For example, a group may look advantaged along two separate dimensions, while people at their intersection remain disadvantaged. The broader goal is to understand how the many pieces of who we are combine to shape not only who connects with whom, but also who gains access to social capital.
